As a result, during America's first century, regulating entry into the country was a power left up to individual states. Apart from laws dealing with immigrant naturalization, Congress concerned itself with only a few modest regulations of the conditions aboard passenger ships traveling to the United States. The states would decide who could enter their ports, and their laws on that front dealt almost entirely with the exclusion of three types of individuals: criminals, paupers, and people suffering from contagious diseases.
